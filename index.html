Week 4: Cell Structure

Characteristics of Living Organisms

All living organisms share a set of common characteristics:

  • Composed of one or more cells
  • Reproduce using deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA)
  • Obtain energy from their environment to support metabolism
  • Sense and respond to their environment
  • Maintain homeostasis (internal stability)
  • Capable of evolution as a group

Cells: The Smallest Unit of Life

A cell is composed of an aqueous interior enclosed in a lipid-based plasma membrane.
  • Cytoplasm — thick, gel-like fluid containing ions and biomolecules
  • Organelle — a specific structure that performs a unique function in the cell
  • Nucleus — houses DNA, enveloped by a double membrane
  • Mitochondria (singular: mitochondrion) — provide energy that fuels all cellular activities
  • Ribosomes — important for protein manufacturing

The Plasma Membrane

  • Separates the cell's interior from its surrounding environment
  • Facilitates chemical processes by concentrating needed raw materials in a limited space
  • Made of a phospholipid bilayer with embedded proteins

Membrane Protein Functions

  • Transport of small molecules
  • Receptors for cell signaling
  • Adhesion to other cells
Fluid Mosaic Model: proteins drift within the plane of the phospholipid bilayer

Internal Compartments of Eukaryotic Cells

Eukaryotic cells are highly structured, efficient, energy-dependent factories capable of reproducing themselves.

Endomembrane System

  • Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)
    • Rough ER — studded with ribosomes; synthesizes proteins
    • Smooth ER — lipid synthesis, detoxification, calcium storage
  • Golgi Apparatus — modifies, sorts, and packages proteins and lipids for transport
  • Lysosomes — contain digestive enzymes; break down waste and cellular debris
  • Peroxisomes — break down fatty acids and detoxify harmful substances (e.g., hydrogen peroxide)
  • Vesicles — transport materials between organelles

Energy Organelles

  • Mitochondria — "powerhouse of the cell"; site of cellular respiration (ATP production); has a double membrane with inner folds called cristae
  • Chloroplasts (plants/algae only) — site of photosynthesis; contain chlorophyll; have thylakoid membranes and stroma
Both mitochondria and chloroplasts have their own DNA — supports the endosymbiont theory!

Cytoskeleton

  • Microfilaments (actin) — cell movement, shape, muscle contraction
  • Intermediate filaments — structural support, anchoring organelles
  • Microtubules (tubulin) — cell division (spindle fibers), intracellular transport, cilia/flagella
  • Centrosomes/Centrioles — organize microtubules during cell division (animal cells)

Week 5: Membranes & Cell Division

The Plasma Membrane as Gate and Gatekeeper

  • Made up of a double layer of lipids — the phospholipid bilayer
  • Phospholipids have a hydrophilic (water-loving) head and hydrophobic (water-fearing) tails
  • Proteins embedded in the bilayer perform transport, signaling, and adhesion
  • Also contains cholesterol (stabilizes fluidity) and carbohydrates (cell recognition)
  • The membrane is selectively permeable — only certain substances can pass
  • Cells expend large amounts of energy to maintain the chemical difference between interior and exterior
The selectively permeable membrane ensures the cell interior is chemically different from the extracellular environment.

Passive Transport (No Energy Required)

Simple Diffusion

  • Passive transport of a substance from a region of higher concentration → lower concentration
  • Continues until equilibrium is reached
  • Smaller molecules diffuse faster than larger ones
  • Heat increases the rate of diffusion
  • Greater concentration difference = faster diffusion rate
  • Small, nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂) can cross the membrane directly

Osmosis

Osmosis = diffusion of WATER across a selectively permeable membrane from lower solute → higher solute concentration
  • Does NOT involve the movement of solute — only water moves
  • Vital for maintaining proper water balance in every cell
  • Water moves toward the side with MORE solute (more concentrated)

Tonicity

Solution TypeSolute ComparisonWater MovementEffect on Cell
HypertonicHigher solute outsideWater leaves cellCell shrivels (crenation/plasmolysis)
HypotonicLower solute outsideWater enters cellCell swells (may lyse)
IsotonicEqual soluteNo net movementCell stays the same
Know hypertonic vs. hypotonic vs. isotonic — very testable!
Plant cells in a hypotonic solution become turgid (firm) thanks to their cell wall. In a hypertonic solution they undergo plasmolysis (membrane pulls away from wall).

Facilitated Diffusion

  • Uses transport proteins (channels or carriers) to move substances down their concentration gradient
  • Still passive — no energy input required
  • Needed for large, polar, or charged molecules that can't cross the lipid bilayer alone (e.g., glucose, ions)
  • Channel proteins — form pores (e.g., aquaporins for water, ion channels)
  • Carrier proteins — change shape to shuttle molecules across

Active Transport (Energy Required)

  • Moves substances against the concentration gradient (low → high)
  • Requires ATP energy
  • Example: Sodium-Potassium Pump (Na+/K+ ATPase) — pumps 3 Na+ out and 2 K+ in per ATP

Bulk Transport

  • Endocytosis — cell takes in large molecules/particles by engulfing them
    • Phagocytosis ("cell eating") — solid particles (e.g., white blood cells engulfing bacteria)
    • Pinocytosis ("cell drinking") — dissolved substances/fluids
    • Receptor-mediated endocytosis — specific molecules bind receptors, triggering uptake
  • Exocytosis — cell releases large molecules by fusing vesicles with the membrane (e.g., neurotransmitter release)

Cell Division

The Cell Cycle

PhaseWhat Happens
G1 (Gap 1)Cell grows, performs normal functions, prepares for DNA synthesis
S (Synthesis)DNA replication occurs — each chromosome duplicated into sister chromatids
G2 (Gap 2)Cell prepares for division; organelles duplicate, proteins synthesized
M (Mitosis)Nuclear division + cytokinesis
G1 + S + G2 = Interphase (the cell spends ~90% of its time here)
G0 phase: Some cells exit the cycle and enter a non-dividing state (e.g., nerve cells, muscle cells).

Mitosis (Somatic Cell Division)

Produces 2 identical daughter cells (diploid → diploid, 2n → 2n)

PhaseKey Events
ProphaseChromatin condenses into visible chromosomes; spindle forms from centrosomes; nuclear envelope begins to break down
MetaphaseChromosomes line up at the metaphase plate (cell equator); spindle fibers attach to centromeres
AnaphaseSister chromatids separate and move to opposite poles; cell elongates
TelophaseNuclear envelopes reform; chromosomes decondense; spindle disassembles
CytokinesisCytoplasm divides — cleavage furrow (animals) or cell plate (plants)
Remember: PMAT — Prophase, Metaphase, Anaphase, Telophase

Meiosis (Gamete Formation)

Produces 4 genetically unique haploid cells (diploid → haploid, 2n → n)

Meiosis has TWO rounds of division: Meiosis I (separates homologous pairs) and Meiosis II (separates sister chromatids)

Meiosis I (Reductional Division)

  • Prophase I — Homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis) forming tetrads; crossing over occurs (exchange of genetic material between non-sister chromatids)
  • Metaphase I — Homologous pairs (tetrads) line up at the metaphase plate; orientation is random (independent assortment)
  • Anaphase I — Homologous chromosomes separate (NOT sister chromatids); one from each pair goes to each pole
  • Telophase I & Cytokinesis — Two haploid cells formed, each with one set of chromosomes (still as sister chromatids)

Meiosis II (Equational Division — similar to mitosis)

  • Sister chromatids separate → 4 haploid daughter cells
  • No additional DNA replication between meiosis I and II

Week 6: Metabolism

Enzymes

Enzymes are proteins that act as biological catalysts — they speed up reactions without being changed themselves.
  • Substrate = the reactant(s) that an enzyme acts on
  • Active site = the region on the enzyme where the substrate binds
  • Product = what is produced after the enzymatic reaction
  • Enzyme-substrate complex = temporary association when substrate binds the active site

Properties of Enzymes

  • Highly specific (each enzyme catalyzes a particular reaction) — lock and key or induced fit model
  • Reusable — not consumed in the reaction
  • Lower the activation energy (energy barrier) needed for a reaction to proceed
  • Affected by temperature (too high = denatures), pH (optimal range varies), and substrate concentration
  • Can be regulated by inhibitors and activators

Enzyme Regulation

  • Competitive inhibitor — binds to the active site (competes with substrate); can be overcome by adding more substrate
  • Noncompetitive (allosteric) inhibitor — binds elsewhere, changing enzyme shape so substrate can't bind effectively
  • Feedback inhibition — end product of a pathway inhibits an earlier enzyme in that same pathway (negative feedback loop)
  • Cofactors (inorganic, e.g., metal ions) and coenzymes (organic, e.g., vitamins) — helper molecules that assist enzyme function
Denaturation: Extreme heat or pH unfolds the enzyme, destroying the active site shape permanently.

Metabolic Pathways

  • Sequences of chemical reactions catalyzed by enzymes
  • Multistep pathways proceed rapidly because enzymes are arranged close together
  • Products of one reaction act as substrates for the next
  • Enzymes may be concentrated in organelles (e.g., mitochondrion), localized in membranes, or clustered in multienzyme complexes
  • Catabolic pathways — break down molecules, release energy (e.g., cellular respiration)
  • Anabolic pathways — build up molecules, require energy (e.g., photosynthesis)

Molecular Energy Carriers

  • Organic molecules necessary for receiving, storing, and delivering energy within the cell
  • ATP (Adenosine Triphosphate) — the most widely used energy carrier
    • Composed of adenine + ribose + 3 phosphate groups
    • Releases energy when it loses a terminal phosphate group → becomes ADP + Pi
    • The bond between the 2nd and 3rd phosphate is a high-energy bond
    • Recycled: ADP + Pi + energy → ATP
  • NADH and NADPH — electron carriers that store energy in loosely bound electrons and hydrogen ions (protons)
  • FADH₂ — another electron carrier used in cellular respiration
  • These deliver electrons to the electron transport chain for high-yield ATP production

Photosynthesis

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
  • Only producers (autotrophs: plants, algae, certain bacteria) carry out photosynthesis
  • Occurs in chloroplasts
  • Chloroplasts contain chlorophyll — a green pigment that absorbs light energy
  • Converts light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose

Light-Dependent Reactions

  • Occur in the thylakoid membranes
  • Light is absorbed by chlorophyll and other pigments (carotenoids, etc.)
  • Water is split (photolysis) → releases O₂ as a byproduct
  • Produce ATP and NADPH (energy carriers for the Calvin Cycle)
  • Involve two photosystems (Photosystem II and Photosystem I) and an electron transport chain

Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent / "Dark" Reactions)

  • Occurs in the stroma of the chloroplast
  • Uses ATP and NADPH from light reactions
  • Fixes CO₂ into organic molecules (G3P, which is used to build glucose)
  • Carbon fixation by the enzyme RuBisCO (most abundant enzyme on Earth)
  • Does NOT directly require light, but depends on products of light reactions

Wavelengths and Color

  • Short wavelengths (violet/blue) have more energy than long wavelengths (red)
  • The color of an object is determined by the wavelengths of visible light it reflects
  • Chlorophyll absorbs red and blue light, reflects green
  • Accessory pigments (carotenoids) absorb other wavelengths → appear yellow/orange

Cellular Respiration

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + ATP (energy)
Cellular respiration is essentially the REVERSE of photosynthesis. Together they form a cycle.
  • All eukaryotes and most bacteria perform cellular respiration
  • Extracts chemical energy from food molecules (primarily glucose)
  • Aerobic respiration requires oxygen

Three Stages of Cellular Respiration

StageLocationInputOutputATP Yield
1. GlycolysisCytoplasm1 Glucose (6C)2 Pyruvate (3C), 2 NADH2 ATP (net)
2. Krebs Cycle (Citric Acid Cycle)Mitochondrial matrix2 Acetyl-CoA (2C)CO₂, NADH, FADH₂2 ATP
3. Electron Transport Chain + Oxidative PhosphorylationInner mitochondrial membraneNADH, FADH₂H₂O~34 ATP
Total yield: ~36–38 ATP per glucose molecule. Glycolysis is the ONLY stage that does NOT require oxygen!
Transition step: Between glycolysis and Krebs, pyruvate is converted to Acetyl-CoA (+ CO₂ + NADH) in the mitochondrial matrix.

Anaerobic Respiration (Fermentation)

  • Occurs when oxygen is absent
  • Begins with glycolysis (same as aerobic), then takes an alternative path to regenerate NAD+
  • Lactic acid fermentation — in muscle cells and some bacteria; pyruvate → lactic acid
  • Alcoholic fermentation — in yeast and some bacteria; pyruvate → ethanol + CO₂
  • Only produces 2 ATP total (from glycolysis alone — much less efficient than aerobic)
